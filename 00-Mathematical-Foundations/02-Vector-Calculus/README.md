# Vector Calculus for Physics

Vector calculus provides the mathematical framework for describing how physical quantities vary across space and time. While linear algebra focuses on discrete structures and transformations, vector calculus extends these ideas to continuous fields, which form the foundation of many physical theories.

In physics, most fundamental quantities are not isolated values but fields—functions defined over space that encode information such as temperature, velocity, or electromagnetic forces. Scalar fields describe magnitude-only quantities, while vector fields capture both magnitude and direction.

The key operators of vector calculus—gradient, divergence, and curl—describe different aspects of how fields behave locally:

* The gradient measures how a scalar field changes in space
* The divergence quantifies sources and sinks within a field
* The curl captures local rotational behavior

These local descriptions are connected to global physical behavior through integral theorems such as Green’s, Stokes’, and Gauss’ theorems. These results reveal a deep principle: the behavior of a field within a region is intrinsically linked to what happens on its boundary.

From a computational perspective, vector calculus plays a central role in numerical simulations. Continuous fields are discretized into grids, and differential operators are approximated to model physical systems such as fluid flow, heat transfer, and electromagnetism. Many modern computational methods are built upon these principles.

This section develops the essential concepts of vector calculus with an emphasis on physical interpretation, geometric intuition, and computational relevance.
