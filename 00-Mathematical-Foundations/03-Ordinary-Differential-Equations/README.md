# Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)

Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) provide the mathematical framework for describing how physical systems evolve in time. While vector calculus focuses on how quantities vary across space, ODEs capture how systems change, making them fundamental to understanding dynamics in physics.

In physical systems, the present state determines the rate of change. An ODE expresses this idea by relating a quantity to its derivative. From motion under forces to oscillations, circuits, and decay processes, ODEs act as the governing laws of time evolution.

Geometrically, an ODE defines a flow in phase space, where each point represents a possible state and the equation assigns a direction of motion. Solutions appear as trajectories that follow this flow, allowing us to visualize system behavior without explicitly solving the equation.

The structure of ODEs reveals different types of dynamics. First-order equations describe relaxation and decay, while second-order equations introduce inertia, leading to oscillations and resonance. Systems of ODEs extend this to coupled dynamics, where multiple variables interact and evolve together.

From a computational perspective, most ODEs cannot be solved analytically. Instead, time is discretized and the system is evolved step-by-step using numerical methods. This transforms continuous physical laws into computable models, where stability and method choice determine the accuracy and realism of simulations.

This section develops ODEs with an emphasis on physical interpretation, geometric intuition, and computational relevance.
